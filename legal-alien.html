I'm a Legal Alien

On feeling foreign and starting anyway

Personal observations · March 2026 · Austin

Spending 12 days abroad alone, in a country that uses characters versus recognizable letter forms, comes with moments of self doubt. On the first day I monkey-see-monkey-do'd my way into ordering food from a touch screen display on the street before entering the restaurant of individually divided seats to eat. As I ate I swiveled my head around to see what to do with my used dishes. I felt uncomfortable. Partly to seem rude, but also to seem weak. I thought about this for a second and figured maybe it is evolutionary, less survival of the fittest than standing out as a potential candidate for elimination of the weakest. But this was far from life and death.

Just start

Growing up I moved frequently and at one point switched from one competitive soccer team to another. I was the worst player. My confidence tanked. I quit trying at practice. It was a downward spiral. When I rejoined my school team, I was better than average by comparison, willing to try and fail, and as a result, got better. Everything out there says learn by doing, shots on goal, build in public. That's what this writing and posting is for me. I could easily spend 100 hours editing a single blog post and never post it, I have years of these. Instead, I'm now trying to produce based on 10 to 20 minutes of focus as often as possible with AI support. Will it be deeply novel? Probably not. Will it help me get better? Yes.

Mile one is the hardest

Participation matters and you simply have to start. Motivation peaks from there. I don't want to go on a run. One mile in I'm annoyed. Two, ok. Three, fine. By mile seven I'm happy. I'm training for a marathon and can run 13+ miles below my zone 4 heart rate, but mile one is still the hardest.

Broaden the range

You may never find "your thing." You may be largely average or frankly bad at most. But you'll never regret trying, and you'll always be better for broadening your range. A few classes I've taken of late:

  • Bonsai (was told this is for grandpas)
  • Sushi and ramen making (I hardly cook)
  • Calligraphy (I'm left handed and it's designed to go the other way)
  • No-gi jiu jitsu (wrestled men with face tattoos on a Friday night)

I may never do some of these again, but the neural plasticity forming as a result is more than enough reward.

Don't evade discipline

Commit for a while to at least a few things. This is just to show and prove to yourself a growth mindset. A lot of things you'll get better at quite rapidly, even after maybe 3 to 7 sessions. I've found this with yoga and it's quite empowering. I played piano for 10 years because my mom said one day you'll be 30 and want to sit down at a party and play. That happened once and it was actually pretty cool.

Bottom line

Let yourself be weird and out of place. No one really cares, except for you if you waste your one precious life. Idleness is more painful than growth.
