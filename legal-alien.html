I'm a Legal Alien

On feeling foreign and starting anyway

Personal observations · March 14, 2026 · Austin

On my first day in Japan I sat in a small restaurant staring at my empty bowl, unsure what to do with it. I had already monkey-see-monkey-do'd my way through ordering from a touchscreen kiosk outside and taken a seat in one of the individually partitioned booths inside. When I finished eating, though, I realized I had no idea where the dishes went. So I just sat there swiveling my head around the room trying to quietly observe what everyone else was doing. I felt uncomfortable. Partly because I did not want to seem rude, but also because I did not want to seem weak. That instinct is probably evolutionary. Not quite survival of the fittest so much as the fear of standing out as a candidate for elimination of the weakest. Of course in this case the stakes were just a bowl of ramen.

Just start

Growing up I moved frequently and at one point switched from one competitive soccer team to another where I was suddenly the worst player. My confidence tanked. I stopped trying at practice and it became a downward spiral. Later I rejoined my school team and by comparison I was above average again. I was willing to try things and fail publicly, and as a result I improved.

Most advice out there says the same thing. Learn by doing. Shots on goal. Build in public. That is what this writing is for me. I could easily spend 100 hours editing a single blog post and never publish it. I have years of drafts like that. Instead I am experimenting with something simpler. Write in 10 to 20 minute bursts, publish, and use AI support to help clean things up. Will it be deeply novel? Probably not. Will it help me get better? Yes.

Mile one is the hardest

Participation matters and you simply have to start. Motivation tends to follow action rather than the other way around. I rarely want to start a run. One mile in I am annoyed. Two miles in I am tolerating it. Three miles in I am fine. By mile seven I am actually happy. Right now I am training for a marathon and can run 13 or more miles below my zone 4 heart rate, but mile one is still the hardest.

Broaden the range

You may never find "your thing." You may end up average, or even bad, at most things. But you will rarely regret trying and you will almost always be better for broadening your range. Some classes I have taken recently include bonsai, which I was told is mostly for grandpas, sushi and ramen making even though I hardly cook, calligraphy despite being left handed when the strokes move the opposite direction, and no-gi jiu jitsu where I wrestled men with face tattoos on a Friday night. I may never do some of these again, but the neural plasticity forming as a result feels like more than enough reward.

Do not evade discipline

At the same time it is worth committing to a few things for a while, long enough to prove to yourself that growth is possible. A surprising number of skills improve quickly once you put a few repetitions in. Sometimes it only takes three to seven sessions to notice real progress. I have felt this with yoga and it is empowering. I also played piano for ten years because my mom used to say that one day you will be 30 and sit down at a party and play. That happened exactly once, and it was actually pretty cool.

Bottom line

Let yourself be weird and out of place. No one really cares nearly as much as you think they do. The only person who will care in the long run is you, if you spend your life avoiding the discomfort of trying things. Idleness ends up hurting more than growth.
