Play to Perform

On marathon training, weekend mornings, and what play actually means

Personal observations · Austin · March 15, 2026

I'm training for a marathon. I know it's a cliche. But when you leave the gym early on a weekend morning, something shifts. The day feels open. You put in effort and are rewarded almost immediately with endorphins and the quiet satisfaction of having done something difficult before most people are awake. It's hard to dismiss someone trying to become the best version of themselves, however "cringe" visible self-improvement can appear. Often the criticism says more about the observer than the person doing the work.

The Weekend Morning Effect

Walking home before noon, I find myself thinking about how many weekend mornings have looked different. Even in adulthood the supply of alcohol and parties remains constant and abundant. You don't want to be no fun, but staying out late and drinking what is essentially a small dose of poison becomes harder to justify over time.

Work Hard, Play Hard

As a kid I was strongly against alcohol and thought I would never drink. Then I submitted all my college applications on New Year's Eve of senior year and got drunk at a party for the first time. It was embarrassing but also marked the beginning of a lifestyle pattern common to many young adults. At the time I convinced myself I needed to become more normal. I had worked extremely hard to get into competitive schools and sacrificed what felt like typical teenage experiences along the way. I started to believe that working hard and playing hard naturally belonged together.

The problem is that the usual interpretation of "work hard, play hard," where the playing part means partying, is mostly negative. Partying is rarely restorative. It consumes energy rather than renewing it.

What Play Actually Is

By contrast, playing sports, being outside, spending time with animals or kids, or even making things like sketches or sculptures feels closer to real play. These activities are enjoyable in themselves and often make the work part easier. Stepping outside the immediate problem space is one of the most reliable ways to return with clearer thinking.

When play shifts from partying to something more literal, the meaning of the phrase changes entirely. Instead of pushing yourself to the limit and escaping afterward, the goal becomes showing up fully across different parts of life. Mental, physical, emotional. Work and play stop competing and begin reinforcing each other.

Surrogate Goals

Achieving surrogate goals like running a marathon builds a kind of discipline that carries into other pursuits. The same energy that helps someone run 26 miles can help them build a company, write consistently, or sustain long periods of creative work. The reverse relationship probably exists as well. A night out that drains the following day likely affects the systems you are trying to build, but it is harder to romanticize.

The way we choose to play quietly shapes the way we perform.
