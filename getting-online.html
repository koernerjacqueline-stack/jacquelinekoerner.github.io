Our Guides Forgot the Foyer

A destination starts a journey

Case study · Tiny experiments · March 8, 2026

People act toward goals. Most of us can explain at any given time why we are where we are and what we are trying to achieve. When we can't tell whether we're making progress, frustration sets in. We might be wasting our time, or heading in the wrong direction entirely.

Imagine an alien visitor asks: how do I get food? The guide replies: go to the store. They go, stand in the aisle, and have no idea what to do next. They might assume they are incompetent, because why didn't the guide prepare them for this?

The guide could have told them all the steps upfront. Instead, they sent an alien into a human designed process with no map.

Lost without a map

I'm working to use new AI tools more, and today exploring how to commit portfolio updates to GitHub using Cursor, a local text editor with AI. Instructions come in sequence: install Command Line Tools, run git init, add a remote, commit, push. Each step eventually works, but none of them connect. Our guides answer questions when asked, but none orient by default. Only when we ask for the big picture do we get it:

Most of us won't ask, because it takes energy while failing to make obvious progress. We'll try to figure it out on our own first, despite coming to a guide for help.

This isn't about being a novice. Even if we go to the theater every week, we still get the program. Even if we've lived in a neighborhood for years, we still look for street signs.

Orientation isn't a sign of inexperience, it's how navigation works. Seeking a guide isn't incompetence, it's how we learn.

You always have to navigate

Why do our AI assistants, designed to explain and support, largely skip onboarding and only tell us what's directly in front of us?

Agents should be partners, not just executors. We wouldn't hire someone with different expertise and blindly trust them without oversight. We'd want to see progress, understand decisions along the way, and have them upskill us so knowledge doesn't live in one place.

Familiarity shouldn't be assumed, it should be built.

Checkpoints change everything

Edit locally, commit, push, live. Four steps. Thirty seconds to explain.

Ask for the map before the directions. Starting on a journey without a map when one exists is making the simple hard.

The world is changing fast, and we should try to take as many people along with us on the AGI journey as possible. We do that best by using classic design patterns that have always helped us move confidently from point A to point B. Orient first. The steps follow.
